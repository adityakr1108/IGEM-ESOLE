Overview

Our feet, often overlooked, are exposed daily to moisture, warmth, and the tight confines of shoes—perfect conditions for bacteria and fungi to thrive. In places like India, over 10 million people are affected by fungal infections annually. E.soleioffers a proactive solution to foot health by preventing infections before they start, rather than treating them after they occur. The bio-active insole, made from a polymer bed hosting bio-engineered bacteria, releases linalool, a fragrant compound that inhibits harmful microbes. Unlike traditional foot creams or antifungal drugs, E.solei works continuously and can be adapted for other areas prone to infection, such as the groin or prosthetic sites.


The idea for E.solei was born from personal experience and community feedback. One of our team members, struggled with a persistent foot infection, and was forced to miss crucial lab meetings and brainstorming sessions due to another bout of foot fungal infection. This made us realize that foot health was not a minor inconvenience but a significant health issue that demanded attention. Through conversations with dermatologists, foot specialists, athletes, and others, we realized that current treatments were often inadequate. These insights led us to develop E.solei, an accessible and effective product designed to tackle foot health challenges before they become serious problems.


How We Meet E Solei

Our approach goes beyond just scientific innovation. We’re focused on enhancing human conditions by addressing real-world challenges through synthetic biology. By engaging with different communities, we’ve ensured that E.solei is both scientifically sound and socially relevant. This localized strategy demonstrates how synthetic biology can provide lasting, meaningful solutions that improve lives globally, showing that science can do more than advance knowledge—it can make a real-world impact.

The REARIE Cycle: A Thoughtful Approach to Problem Solving

The REARIE Cycle—which stands for Research, Engage, Analyze, Refine, Implement, and Evaluate—provides a structured framework that has guided us throughout the development of E.solei. Each phase of the cycle allowed us to reflect on feedback, continuously improve the product, and ensure that our decisions align with both our scientific goals and the well-being of our end users.

Knowing The Problem

We took a comprehensive approach by conducting in-depth interviews with dermatologists and foot specialists to tap into expert knowledge, while also circulating a survey to a diverse group—ranging from army personnel and athletes to students. This combination of expert insight and real-world experiences provided us with a well-rounded understanding of the widespread issue of foot infections and the limitations of current treatments. These valuable perspectives were instrumental in shaping the innovative design and purpose of E.solei.

Dermatologists and Diabetic Foot Care Specialist

Dr. Aparna Palit

Dermatologist, AIIMS, Kalyani

On June 11th, 2024, the iGEM Team from IISER Kolkata had the opportunity to interview Dr. Aparna Palit, a dermatologist at AIIMS, Kalyani. The interview focused on fungal and bacterial foot infections prevalent in the locality, based on recent patient records.

Key Takeaways

"We commonly encounter two types of fungal foot infections: Candida sp. and dermatophyte infections. Candidiasis is prevalent among people working in moist, muddy conditions, while dermatophyte infections are more common in industrial workers who wear closed shoes for long periods. Environmental factors, like keeping pets at home, can increase exposure to foot fungus, and commensal bacteria often cause foot odor."

Team Reflection and Intervention

The interview with Dr. Palit provided us with crucial insights into the prevalence, causes, and treatment of fungal foot infections, particularly in high-humidity environments like India. Understanding the specific infections (Candida and dermatophytes) and the environmental risks (such as prolonged exposure to moisture and pet-related fungal exposure) allowed us to refine our approach.

Implementation in Our Project

  • Understanding Common Fungal Infections: Based on Dr. Palit's advice, we learned about the common Candida species and dermatophyte infections, which are prevalent among individuals working in moist environments while wearing shoes.
  • Education on Safe Remedies: We identified several misconceptions about home remedies for these infections. In our outreach, we made sure to incorporate information addressing these misconceptions, educating people on the correct treatments.
  • Risk Factors: As suggested by Dr. Palit, we will also raise awareness about environmental and lifestyle factors, such as the impact of keeping pets and the importance of proper foot care in humid conditions.

Dr. Ghanashyam Goyal

Diabetic Foot Care Specialist, ILS Hospitals, Salt Lake

“Treat your feet just like you treat your face”

The iGEM 2024 Team from IISER Kolkata interviewed Dr. Ghanashyam Goyal to gain insights into the prevalence of fungal and bacterial infections among diabetic patients, the severity of these infections, preventive measures, and strategies to market our product in the diabetic population.

Key Takeaways

The main causes of fungal infections in diabetic patients include walking barefoot, neglecting foot health, and untreated open wounds. Diabetic patients with neuropathy are particularly vulnerable due to compromised nerve function, which is why custom-made shoes with softer insoles are essential for providing stress relief and preventing infections. In severe cases, foot infections can lead to necrosis and even amputation, so it's crucial to maintain proper blood sugar control, have regular check-ups, and ensure good foot care—such as not walking barefoot and using customized insoles. To reach the diabetic customer segment, I recommend partnering with hospitals to distribute customized insoles and assess their effectiveness. You can conduct randomized control trials, where diabetic patients receive either customized or normal insoles, and monitor their susceptibility to infections over 6 months to 1 year to evaluate the product's benefits.

Team Reflection and Intervention

The interview provided us with critical insights into the heightened vulnerability of diabetic patients to foot infections and reinforced the importance of prevention and proper foot care. Dr. Goyal’s suggestions on distributing our product through hospitals and conducting trials have shaped our approach to targeting the diabetic population and refining our product’s design and functionality.

Implementation in Our Project

  • Focus on Diabetic Patients: In addition to focusing on workers and the general public, we have gained new insights into designing insoles specifically tailored for diabetic neuropathic patients, who are more prone to foot fungal infections.
  • Collaborative Marketing: We plan to partner with hospitals to introduce our product to diabetic patients, allowing us to reach a larger number of patients.

Survey Form Insights

To gather valuable insights for our E. Solei Project, we conducted a survey targeting diverse groups, including students, athletes, office workers, and army personnel. The survey explored common foot issues like fungal infections and odor, along with footwear habits and preferences for foot care products. We also gauged public interest in using genetically modified organisms (GMOs) in foot care, assessing comfort levels and concerns. This feedback is crucial in refining our product to meet real needs while promoting foot health and sustainability. Key areas explored include Demographic Insights, Understanding Foot Issues, Consumer Preferences, and Perceptions of Genetically Modified Bacteria in Footcare. With 200 responses collected through academic institutions and social media, this feedback provides essential guidance for developing innovative, eco-friendly, and effective footcare solutions that align with consumer expectations.


Survey Result Analysis


  1. 1. Demographic Insights: Our survey revealed a diverse group of participants, with 61% being students, followed by 13.8% office workers. The majority (62.7%) are aged 18-35, showing strong interest from younger adults. In terms of income, 41.9% of respondents come from middle-income households (4-10 lakh). Geographically, nearly half (49.7%) live in urban areas, with a balanced representation from rural and semi-urban regions. This mix gives us valuable insights into the varying foot care needs across different lifestyles and demographics!


  2. 2 Understanding Foot Issues and the Demand for Solutions: From the survey on foot-related issues, we learned that the majority of respondents (60.5%) commonly use open sandals or flip-flops, followed by casual shoes (52.3%). Interestingly, 35.9% reported frequently experiencing foot odor after long hours of wearing shoes, with 38.8% wearing closed shoes for 5-8 hours daily. Notably, 41.8% of participants have faced foot-related issues like redness and itchiness, with 50% of these individuals mentioning itchiness as the most common problem, alongside dry skin (42.5%) and blisters (35%). Additionally, over half of the respondents (53.1%) who had fungal infections reported recurrence after treatment. This feedback confirms the relevance of our project in addressing common issues such as odor, itchiness, and recurring infections.


  3. 3. Consumer Preferences and Product Expectations: From the survey results, we learned that the most desired benefits from a footcare product are protection against fungal infections (81.3%), elimination of foot odor (75%), and moisturizing and softening the skin (63.7%). Additionally, 91.3% of respondents prefer a single product that offers multiple benefits rather than separate products for each issue. The importance of scent was also highlighted, with 66.3% finding it either very or somewhat important. Awareness about the environmental impact of fungal creams was moderate, with 35% aware of their potential negative effects. Interestingly, while 31.3% would definitely pay extra for eco-friendly materials, the majority (62.5%) would consider it depending on the price. This indicates a strong demand for an all-in-one footcare solution with a focus on effectiveness, pleasant scent, and sustainability, as long as the price remains reasonable.


  4. 4. Perceptions of Genetically Modified Bacteria in Footcare: From the survey results, we found that familiarity with genetically modified organisms (GMOs) is fairly balanced, with 32% of respondents being very familiar and 36.6% somewhat familiar with their use in biotechnology. Comfort with the idea of using GMOs in everyday applications, such as footcare, showed a split, with 30.2% feeling very comfortable, while 29.7% were somewhat comfortable. A notable 41.2% of respondents have previously used probiotic products, indicating some level of familiarity with beneficial bacteria. Safety concerns topped the list, with 69.8% of participants worried about the potential risks to humans, followed by concerns about effectiveness (52.1%) and environmental impact (44.8%). Interestingly, the majority indicated that assurances of clinical safety (75%), regulatory certification (65.1%), and transparent product information (68.2%) would significantly increase their comfort levels in using footcare products containing GMOs. This indicates a clear need for educating consumers about the safety, efficacy, and sustainability of using GMOs in personal care products.

Discussion with Border Security Forces on Footwear and Foot Health

"फ़ौजी लोगों का सबसे नाज़ुक हिस्सा उनका पैर होता है।"

"The most vulnerable part of soldiers is their feet."

We had the opportunity to interview personnel from the Border Security Force (BSF) stationed at a camp near IISER-Kolkata, including a Deputy Commandant and a Quarter Commandant. The purpose of our visit was to gather insights into the footwear usage and foot health concerns of soldiers, as we initially believed that army personnel who wear shoes for extended periods would be our key stakeholders.

Important Dialogue

Deputy Commandant: In the first few months of training, it's quite common to see issues like boils, redness, and white discoloration on the feet. But over time, the body adjusts, and these problems tend to reduce.

During the discussion, the soldiers also revealed that they use different types of shoes for specific purposes. They shared insights about three key types: Jungle Boots, Combat Boots, and DMS (Direct Moulded Sole) Shoes.

Quarter Commandant: Jungle Boots are the most comfortable for long-range patrols. They're tough and breathable, perfect for rugged terrains, especially in Naxal areas where we might cover up to 30-40 kilometers in a single run.

The conversation also revealed critical information about the challenges faced by soldiers in extreme climates. For instance, in colder regions like Siachen, moisture inside their shoes can freeze into ice crystals, causing severe damage to their feet. Similarly, in hot desert regions like Jaisalmer, soldiers often experience pale skin and boils due to trapped heat inside their shoes.

Soldier recently returned from the border: "Up in Siachen, the moisture in your boots actually freezes into ice. You can feel the crystals forming inside, and it gets so bad that some guys end up with frostbite. In the worst cases, we've even seen amputations because of it."

While soldiers have adapted well to their footwear in more moderate climates, we were surprised to learn that foot care is not formally addressed in their training or routine. Foot-related issues are generally regarded as minor, and only a few soldiers face these problems at any given time. However, the soldiers did stress the importance of insoles for added comfort.

"Insoles are key. They need to be thick and customized depending on the type of shoe we're wearing. It can make all the difference during long patrols."

From our discussion with BSF personnel, we learned that soldiers, especially during early training, face foot issues like boils and redness but eventually adapt to their footwear. Jungle Boots are preferred for their durability and breathability during long-range patrols in rugged terrains, while Combat Boots are less suited for extended wear, emphasizing the need for purpose-specific footwear. However, new recruits and those undergoing intensive training may still benefit from improved footwear options.

Foot health becomes a serious concern in extreme climates. In freezing areas like Siachen, moisture inside shoes can freeze, leading to frostbite and even amputations. In desert regions like Jaisalmer, trapped heat causes boils and skin discoloration. These issues highlight the need for footwear that manages moisture and temperature effectively in extreme environments.

Additionally, soldiers emphasized the importance of thick, customized insoles for long-duration missions. This feedback guided us in refining E. Solei by incorporating different strains of bacteria capable of withstanding extreme climatic conditions, particularly for those operating in high-altitude environments where foot health is most at risk. Additionally, we will need to focus on affordability and value, while broadening our perspective on potential stakeholders to ensure that our solution benefits a wider range of users.

Engagement with Stakeholders and Expert Insights

Stakeholder Feedback

After engaging with our stakeholders, including students, athletes, corporate employees, defense personnel, and the general public, we gained valuable insights that reshaped our project approach. Their feedback provided diverse perspectives on how our bacterial insole technology could fit into their daily lives, focusing on comfort, functionality, and affordability. These conversations gave us a deeper understanding of user needs and allowed us to refine our design accordingly.

Expert Insights

Our project focuses on two key components: the production of linalool—known for its antifungal, wound healing, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and lavender fragrance properties—and the sustained production of linalool using live bacterial cultures. We encountered a significant challenge in controlling the bacterial population to prevent overgrowth, which could compromise the product's longevity. To address this issue, we developed a population control module incorporating a sensing system to regulate bacterial growth, ensuring that the product remains effective over time.
