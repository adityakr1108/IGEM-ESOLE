PRODUCTION:
Lab-Level Production Strategies Bulk-Level Production Strategies
1. Preparation of Competent Cells: Using established techniques, competent E. coli cells are prepared using TFB1, TFB2, and CaCl2-RbCl. Electroporation might improve plasmid DNA absorption. 1. Scale-Up of Competent Cells: For bulk production, large-scale cultures of Lactobacillus rhamnosus are grown to high cell densities. Fermentation techniques optimize growth conditions like nutrient availability and pH.
2. Cotransformation: Two different plasmids are concurrently injected into prepared competent cells to optimize absorption efficiency. 2. Co-transformation in Bioreactors: Conducted in bioreactors for greater control over environmental parameters like mixing and oxygenation.
3. Selection and Screening: Cells are plated on antibiotic-resistant media to select effective colonies using molecular methods. 3. High-Throughput Screening: Rapid identification and isolation of transformed colonies using automated systems for colony picking and PCR validation.
4. Small-Scale Cultivation: Positive colonies are inoculated into small liquid cultures like LB broth to facilitate further growth and analysis. 4. Cryopreservation: Transformed strains are cryopreserved to ensure long-term viability and plasmid stability.
5. Scale-Up Culture Conditions: Optimising culture media and growth conditions in larger fermenters. Continuous monitoring of growth parameters is crucial.
6. Quality Control: Comprehensive quality control measures, including assessments of plasmid stability and gene expression levels.

Packing

Choice of Polymer

Everything you see around you, from your toothbrush bristles to the pen you are holding, is made from a polymer. These large compounds, made of repeating units called monomers, include naturally-occurring polymers like cellulose and natural rubber. Our project, E.solei, uses a bio-compatible polymer infused with nutrients to promote the growth of our genetically modified organism (GMO) and subsequent production of Linalool.

We propose using an alginate-collagen brush polymer, ideal for housing our GMO, Lactobacillus rhamnosus, due to its excellent binding properties and distinguished hydrophobicity.

Composition and Structural Attributes

Alginate, derived from brown algae, is biocompatible, biodegradable, and undergoes rapid ionic gelation. Combined with collagen, it forms a hydrogel that enhances mechanical stability and is less prone to swelling.

The polymer brush we propose will have brush-like extensions to which our GMO will adhere, due to its hydrophobic surface properties.

Packaging and Usage

The product is packaged in two layers, each with a brush polymer and lyophilised bacteria adhered to the surface. Before use, the encasing is removed, and an inducer medium is sprayed to activate the bacteria.

Disposal

Disposal of Product

It is crucial to implement proper disposal mechanisms for the Genetically Modified Organism (GMO) when released outside laboratory conditions. Improper disposal can lead to mutations, including horizontal gene transfer, and potentially cancerous developments or severe allergies, resulting in harmful effects on both humans and other animals. Additionally, environmental hazardous chemicals might be released by mutated organisms due to faulty disposal measures.

To combat these risks, our product, E. solei, incorporates comprehensive disposal strategies for every layer of manufacturing, ensuring safety and minimizing environmental impact.

Disposal

Strategy for Hosting Bed:

Perspective 1: Alginate

The hosting material of the product is composed of Alginate, a hydrocolloid obtained primarily from brown algae. It is non-toxic to tissues and cells, hence poses no risk from skin contact on accidental exposure to the broken product cassette. Alginate can be degraded at high temperatures (above 250°C) and can also be dismantled by cation chelation. Recyclability is ensured through the pH control of the chelator.

Perspective 2: Collagen and Chitosan

Collagen, a major structural component in animals, is completely biodegradable and is degraded by enzymatic actions. It has no harmful effects on exposure to animals or humans. Chitosan, known for its biodegradability and low toxicity, is degraded by Lysozyme enzyme exposure and breaks down into non-toxic residues more quickly in the soil.

Strategy for Structure and GMO

Perspective 3: Brush Polymers

Brush polymers are composed of several polymer chains organized to perform specific functions. These are recycled by exposure to high temperatures, degraded by the action of microorganisms, and are eco-friendly, hence having diverse biomedical applications.

Perspective 4: Lactobacillus rhamnosus

Lactobacillus rhamnosus, a probiotic bacteria, is considered safe for humans and animals. Our proposed kill switch machinery adds extra safety by automatically killing the bacteria upon detachment from its substrate. In its lyophilized form, the bacteria remain inactive until product activation, ensuring safety against unintentional release or breakage.

As every layer of our product is biodegradable or recyclable with no harm to the environment, the materials will be collected in a disposal bag provided with the product packaging, labelled as “recyclable”. This allows for sorted collection by the waste management personnel of the area.
